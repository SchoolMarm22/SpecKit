# What We're Looking For

I need someone who gets excited about Rails at scale — not the Rails you learned in tutorials, but Rails when you have 10M+ merchants depending on your code. We're one of the largest Rails applications in the world, and that comes with problems you won't read about in DHH's blog posts.

Merchant obsession isn't just a value here, it's how we evaluate every technical decision. When you're proposing an architecture change, the first question is always "how does this help merchants make more money?" If you can't answer that, we're not interested. We've seen too many engineers who are great at building technically impressive systems that solve the wrong problems.

Remote-first means you need to be thoughtful about communication and documentation. We don't do drive-by Slack messages or hallway conversations that exclude half the team. Everything important goes in writing, and your code needs to tell a story that someone in a different timezone can follow.

## Must-Haves

- 5+ years with Rails in production, ideally at scale (>1M users)
- React experience with component-driven development (Polaris experience is a bonus, but understanding design systems is the real signal)
- Has shipped features end-to-end, including the database migrations, API changes, and frontend components
- Can articulate the tradeoffs of different Rails patterns (concerns vs service objects vs form objects) based on actual experience
- Strong written communication — we're remote-first, so if you can't explain your thinking asynchronously, you won't be effective here

## Strong Signals

- Has worked on performance optimization at scale (N+1 queries, database indexing, caching strategies)
- Experience with GraphQL (we use it heavily) or strong API design opinions
- Has built or contributed to internal tooling/CLI tools
- Open source Rails contributions or has spoken about Rails at conferences
- Demonstrates merchant empathy — talks about features in terms of business impact, not just technical elegance

## Anti-Patterns

- "Full-stack" experience that was really just gluing together microservices with REST calls
- Can't explain why they chose ActiveRecord over raw SQL (or vice versa) in a specific situation
- Dismisses monoliths as "legacy" without understanding the trade-offs (our modular monolith works, fight me)
- Has never been on-call or responsible for the production impact of their code
- Treats frontend as an afterthought or "just some React components"

## Interview Focus

- Concrete examples of Rails performance problems they've solved and how they diagnosed them
- How they approach breaking down large features across the stack
- Database design decisions — we'll give them a merchant scenario and see how they model it
- Code review philosophy and how they balance craft with shipping velocity
- How they stay current with Rails and frontend ecosystems without chasing every new trend
