# What We're Looking For

I need someone who's been in the trenches building services that actually scale, not just services that could theoretically scale. At Amazon, you own your service end-to-end — you build it, you run it, you get paged when it breaks at 2am on Sunday. If you've never been woken up by your own code, this probably isn't the right fit.

We're looking for engineers who naturally think in terms of customer impact first, then work backwards to technical solutions. The PRFAQ discipline isn't just documentation here — it's how we think. Before you write a single line of code, you should be able to articulate what customer problem you're solving and why your approach is the right one, even when (especially when) other smart people disagree with you.

This is a two-pizza team building foundational AWS services. Your code will run on millions of instances. When you make an architectural decision, it affects AWS customers globally. That level of ownership is exhilarating for the right person and terrifying for everyone else.

## Must-Haves

- Built and operated distributed Java services handling >10k RPS in production
- Owned end-to-end service lifecycle including oncall, monitoring, capacity planning
- Led technical decision with significant blast radius (database choice, framework migration, API design) and can articulate the tradeoffs
- Experience with service mesh or microservice communication patterns at scale
- Demonstrates ownership mentality — has stories of going beyond their job description to solve customer problems

## Strong Signals

- Has driven down operational load through automation or architectural changes
- Experience with AWS services in anger, not just tutorials (understands when DynamoDB is wrong choice)
- Can articulate specific examples of disagreeing with senior engineer/manager and the outcome
- Has built services with strict SLA requirements and the monitoring/alerting to prove it
- Shows frugality in technical decisions — understands cost implications of architectural choices

## Anti-Patterns

- "Microservices" experience that was really just splitting a monolith without understanding service boundaries
- Can't explain a time they were wrong about a technical decision and what they learned
- Thinks oncall is someone else's job or treats operational concerns as afterthoughts
- Makes technical decisions in isolation without considering customer impact
- Has only worked in environments where "someone else handles production"

## Interview Focus

- System design: focus on failure modes, not happy path. How do you handle cascading failures?
- Leadership principles in action: specific examples of ownership, bias for action, diving deep
- Operational excellence: how they've reduced toil, improved reliability, or handled incidents
- Disagree and commit: times they've had to execute on decisions they disagreed with
- Working backwards: can they start from customer need and work backwards to technical solution?
