# What We're Looking For

At Meta, infrastructure is not just keeping the lights on — it's the platform that enables 3 billion people to connect. Our infrastructure team builds systems that Facebook, Instagram, WhatsApp, and our AI initiatives depend on. When we ship something, it affects humanity-scale traffic within hours.

You'll be joining a team that moves fast and breaks things (responsibly). We don't gate-keep with process — we hire smart people and let them figure it out. Our new engineer bootcamp means you'll spend 6 weeks learning our stack before choosing your team, so you need to be someone who thrives in ambiguity and can ramp up quickly on massive codebases.

We open source everything we can (React, PyTorch, Folly, Proxygen) because we believe the best infrastructure emerges from community iteration, not corporate secrecy. If you've never contributed to or maintained an open source project at scale, you'll learn fast here.

## Must-Haves

- Built distributed systems that handle millions of QPS in production (not just theoretical scale)
- Contributed meaningfully to system design decisions that affected how other teams build software
- Experience with C++, Python, or Rust at scale — our infrastructure is performance-critical and memory-efficient by necessity
- Has debugged performance issues in production systems under time pressure
- Can explain the CAP theorem tradeoffs they've actually navigated, not just recite the theory

## Strong Signals

- Open source contributions or maintainership (shows you write code for other engineers to build on)
- Has built developer tools or internal platforms that other engineers adopted
- Experience with Facebook's open source stack (React, GraphQL, Buck, Folly) or equivalent scale systems
- Shipped features during hackathons or equivalent rapid prototyping environments
- Can articulate specific technical decisions where they chose speed over perfection and explain the tradeoffs

## Anti-Patterns

- Process-heavy background where shipping required multiple approval gates (you'll be frustrated by our velocity expectations)
- Can't explain system design decisions they personally made vs. just implemented
- Dismissive of rapid iteration or "move fast" culture as reckless (it's not reckless when done right)
- Only worked on internal tools that never faced external traffic or real user load
- Uncomfortable with ambiguous requirements or changing priorities

## Interview Focus

- System design at Meta scale: how would they handle Instagram Stories traffic spike during New Year's Eve
- Performance debugging: walk through finding a memory leak in a service handling 50M RPS
- Code review of actual Meta-style C++ or Python (we'll share real examples from our codebase)
- Hackathon mindset: how would they prototype and validate a new infrastructure feature in 48 hours
- Cultural fit: examples of times they moved fast, broke something, learned from it, and shipped the fix
