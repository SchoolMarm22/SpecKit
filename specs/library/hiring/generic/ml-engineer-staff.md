# What We're Looking For

I need someone who's moved beyond just training models to thinking about ML systems at scale. Staff level means you're architecting solutions that work for the next 2-3 years, not just the next sprint. You should have opinions about when NOT to use ML — half the problems that land on our desk don't actually need machine learning.

The right person has been through at least one full ML platform migration and can tell war stories about data drift, model versioning nightmares, and serving latency surprises. You've probably built tooling that other ML engineers now take for granted. Most importantly, you can translate between the research team's "this paper looks interesting" and the product team's "why is accuracy down 2%?"

## Must-Haves

- Built and operated ML systems serving >1M predictions/day in production
- Led technical decisions that affected multiple ML teams (platform, tooling, or standards)
- Experience with model versioning, A/B testing, and gradual rollouts at scale
- Can debug performance bottlenecks across the entire ML stack (data → training → serving)
- Has migrated or redesigned a significant ML system while maintaining uptime

## Strong Signals

- Built reusable ML infrastructure that other teams adopted
- Experience with multiple ML frameworks and can articulate when to use which
- Has strong opinions about feature stores, model registries, or ML observability
- Can mentor senior engineers and has grown other ML practitioners
- Understands the business impact of ML decisions, not just technical metrics

## Anti-Patterns

- Only worked on research or prototyping without production deployment
- Treats MLOps as someone else's problem
- Can't explain why they chose their current ML stack over alternatives
- Focuses only on model accuracy without considering latency, cost, or maintainability
- Has never been responsible for model performance degradation in production

## Interview Focus

- System design: how they'd build an ML platform from scratch for a team of 20 engineers
- War stories: production failures they've debugged and architectural decisions they'd make differently
- Technical strategy: how they evaluate and introduce new ML tools or frameworks
- Cross-functional collaboration: how they work with product, engineering, and data teams
- Mentorship philosophy: how they've grown other ML engineers and technical decision-making
