# What We're Looking For

I need someone who's been burned by their own architectural decisions before. Senior means you've built systems that fell over under load, made database choices you regretted six months later, and learned to balance pragmatic delivery with technical excellence.

This isn't a "senior" title because you've been coding for 5+ years. This is senior because you can look at a feature request, immediately think about caching strategy, database impact, and how it affects three other teams. You've been the person other engineers come to when they're stuck, and you've mentored someone from junior to mid-level.

We're looking for an engineer who can own entire problem domains, not just individual tickets. Someone who thinks in systems and can articulate the tradeoffs between different architectural approaches without defaulting to "it depends."

## Must-Haves

- 5+ years building production Python systems with real traffic (>10k daily users)
- Designed and implemented APIs that other teams depend on
- Experience with both SQL and NoSQL databases in production, with opinions about when to use which
- Has been oncall for systems they built and can talk about what broke and why
- Led technical decisions that required buy-in from multiple engineers or teams

## Strong Signals

- Contributed to architectural decisions around data modeling, service boundaries, or performance optimization
- Has mentored junior engineers through complex technical problems
- Experience with async Python (asyncio, Celery, or similar) in production
- Can articulate specific tradeoffs they've made between consistency, availability, and partition tolerance

## Anti-Patterns

- "Senior" experience that was mostly following detailed technical specs written by someone else
- Can't explain why they chose their current tech stack beyond "that's what we used"
- Has never been responsible for system performance or scaling decisions
- Treats code review as just syntax checking rather than architectural discussion
- Always advocates for the newest, shiniest technology without considering operational complexity

## Interview Focus

- System design: how they think about service boundaries, data flow, and failure modes
- Mentoring examples: specific situations where they helped junior engineers grow
- Technical tradeoffs: decisions they've made where they had to choose between competing priorities
- Production experience: outages they've debugged, performance problems they've solved
- Code architecture: how they structure applications for maintainability and testing
