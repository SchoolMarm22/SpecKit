# What We're Looking For

Looking for someone who's evolved past just writing code to thinking about systems and people. At the staff level, your impact comes through the decisions you influence and the technical directions you set, not just the tickets you close.

I want to see evidence of technical leadership that goes beyond "senior engineer who's been around longer." You should have opinions about service boundaries, data consistency models, and how to structure teams around technical architecture. Most importantly, you should have been wrong about some of these opinions and learned from it.

The ideal candidate has lived through at least one major system migration or architecture evolution. Someone who's seen a monolith become microservices, or microservices collapse back into a monolith, and can articulate why either direction made sense given the context.

## Must-Haves

- Designed and executed a major architectural change that impacted multiple teams (service extraction, data migration, etc.)
- Deep JVM expertise: can debug memory issues, tune garbage collection, explain concurrency models beyond basic synchronized blocks
- Has mentored multiple engineers to senior level, with specific examples of growth areas they identified and addressed
- Experience with distributed systems patterns: can explain CAP theorem implications in real scenarios, not just textbook definitions
- Track record of technical decision-making that considers both engineering and business constraints

## Strong Signals

- Has strong opinions about Spring vs. non-Spring approaches and can defend their position with real examples
- Experience with event-driven architectures and can discuss ordering guarantees, idempotency, and failure modes
- Can speak to database scaling strategies they've implemented (sharding, read replicas, caching layers)
- Has deprecated or sunset a significant system they once built or maintained

## Anti-Patterns

- "Staff" experience that was really just senior IC work with a bigger title
- Can't articulate the difference between technical complexity and business complexity
- Dogmatic about technology choices without considering team context or business constraints
- Never been responsible for a production incident or can't discuss a technical decision they'd make differently
- Treats code review as gatekeeping rather than knowledge sharing

## Interview Focus

- System design scenarios that require tradeoff discussions, not just "correct" architectures
- Stories about technical decisions that didn't work out and what they learned
- How they approach technical mentorship and raising the bar for code quality across teams
- Their philosophy on when to introduce new technologies or frameworks to a codebase
- Specific examples of how they've influenced technical direction beyond their immediate team
