# What We're Looking For

I need someone who thinks about frontend architecture the way backend engineers think about distributed systems. At staff level, you're not just building features — you're designing the systems that dozens of engineers will build features on top of for the next 2-3 years.

The ideal candidate has lived through at least one major Angular migration (probably AngularJS to Angular 2+, or a major version upgrade that broke things). They understand that technical decisions at scale aren't just about code quality — they're about team velocity, hiring constraints, and business continuity. I want someone who can look at our current frontend architecture and tell me not just what's wrong, but why it's wrong and what the migration path looks like without stopping feature development.

This role will own technical strategy across multiple frontend teams, mentor senior engineers, and make architecture decisions that affect 20+ engineers. You should be comfortable being wrong in front of smart people and changing course when new information emerges.

## Must-Haves

- 6+ years of Angular experience including at least one major version migration in production
- Designed and implemented frontend architecture that scaled to 50+ engineers working in the same codebase
- Led technical initiatives that crossed team boundaries (shared component libraries, build systems, testing strategies)
- Track record of making large codebases more maintainable, not just adding features to them
- Can articulate specific opinions about state management patterns and when to use RxJS vs simpler alternatives

## Strong Signals

- Has strong, defendable opinions about monorepo vs multi-repo for frontend teams
- Experience with micro-frontend architectures and can explain when they're worth the complexity
- Built or contributed to open source Angular libraries or tools
- Can explain the performance implications of different Angular change detection strategies
- Has mentored engineers from mid-level to senior (we need force multipliers, not just individual contributors)

## Anti-Patterns

- "Angular expert" who hasn't worked with teams larger than 10 engineers
- Treats TypeScript as "JavaScript with types" rather than understanding its impact on architecture
- Can't explain why they chose their state management solution beyond "it's what we used"
- Focuses on code elegance over team productivity and business outcomes
- Has never had to deprecate their own architectural decisions

## Interview Focus

- Architecture stories: describe a frontend architecture decision they owned, how it evolved, what they'd do differently
- Cross-team influence: how they've gotten other teams to adopt their technical standards or tools
- Technical judgment under constraints: how they balance technical debt, feature velocity, and system reliability
- Migration strategy: how they approach large-scale frontend refactoring without stopping feature development
- Mentorship philosophy: how they help senior engineers grow into technical leaders
